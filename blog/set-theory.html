In grade school, there exists a basic progression of mathematics which almost everyone in the United States has experienced. First we learn to count, then do basic arithmetic, slowly introducing more complicated concepts like fractions, negative numbers, solving equations, graphs, et cetera. This is good knowledge, and it is important to know. However, it ever so slightly misses the entire point of math. The point of math is to formalize rational thought, and the central tool for doing so is the set.

The idea of a set is very simple.

If \(G\) is a group and \(N\) is a normal subgroup, then we form the quotient group \[G/N\] whose elements are the cosets of \(N\) in \(G\). This construction appears naturally when studying symmetry and equivalence relations.
